2026 WNBA Draft
In Review — Phoenix Mercury Focus
Draft Intelligence
The Shed at Hudson Yards, New York City · ESPN

The 2026 WNBA Draft delivered a historic night headlined by Azzi Fudd going No. 1 overall to Dallas — reuniting with Paige Bueckers — and UCLA setting records with 6 players drafted (5 in Round 1). Three expansion franchises made their first-ever picks, Golden State's controversial Flau'Jae Johnson trade dominated the post-draft discourse, and the new CBA saw the first $500K rookie contract in WNBA history.

For Phoenix: The Mercury entered the draft without a first-round pick (traded to Connecticut) and selected two international developmental prospects — French wing Inés Pitarch-Granel (No. 27) and Hungarian point guard Eszter Rátkai (No. 42). Both are 19-year-old Europeans with professional experience, continuing GM Nick U'Ren's strategy of investing internationally rather than chasing established names with late-round picks. The real roster building happened before the draft — re-signing Copper, Thomas, Bonner, and Whitcomb to maintain the 2025 Finals core.

Media grades were mixed: ESPN gave a D (hard to evaluate 19-year-old European picks), while Bleacher Report was more optimistic at B-/C, noting Pitarch-Granel's defensive ceiling. The consensus: Phoenix's 2026 success depends on its veteran core, not draft night.

Phoenix held two selections — No. 27 (Round 2) and No. 42 (Round 3) — their first draft picks in two years. The Mercury's original first-round slot (No. 12) was traded to Connecticut in a prior sign-and-trade deal. No trades were made during the draft itself.

UCLA Makes Draft History
Six UCLA players drafted — five in Round 1 (Betts 4, Jaquez 5, Rice 6, Dugalić 9, Kneepkens 15) — setting two WNBA records: most players from one school in a single draft (6) and most first-round picks from one school (5). The 2025-26 national champions sent their entire starting core to the pros.
The Dallas Reunion
For the second straight year, Dallas held the No. 1 pick. In 2025 they took Paige Bueckers (Rookie of the Year). In 2026, they reunited her with UConn teammate and girlfriend Azzi Fudd. The two won the 2025 NCAA title together and now build a potential dynasty in Dallas. Under the new CBA, Fudd earns $500K in Year 1 — the first $500K rookie deal in WNBA history.
Seattle: Draft Night's Biggest Winners
After losing Diggins, G. Williams, and Sykes in free agency, the Storm rebuilt through the draft: Awa Fam (No. 3), Flau'Jae Johnson (No. 8 via trade), Taina Mair (No. 14), Marta Suárez (No. 16). Exceptionally young core with Fam (19), Johnson (22), Mair (22), and Ezi Magbegor (26).
The Flau'Jae Johnson Mystery
Golden State drafted LSU star Flau'Jae Johnson at No. 8 then immediately traded her to Seattle for Suárez + a 2028 second-rounder. Most analysts called it the worst move of draft night. Valkyries GM Nyanin refused to explain: "When I'm ready… I'll speak on it."
Expansion Teams Arrive
All three expansion franchises made Round 1 picks: Toronto Tempo took Kiki Rice (No. 6) — their first-ever pick in franchise history. Portland Fire selected Iyana Martín Carrión (No. 7) from Spain. Golden State's pick (Johnson) was traded. Toronto becomes the first Canadian WNBA franchise.
Mystics Triple Up — But Questions Remain
Washington held three top-11 picks (Betts 4, Dugalić 9, McMahon 11) but drew criticism for bypassing elite shooters despite being the league's worst 3PT team (32.9%). All three selections were bigs or interior-leaning forwards. The team also fired GM Jamila Wideman citing "serious strategic differences" before the draft.
New CBA Transforms the Stakes
First draft under the new CBA. No. 1 pick now earns $500K (up from $74K). Average WNBA salary rose to ~$583K. Supermax at $1.4M in 2026, rising to $2.4M. New 11-year broadcast deals worth ~$2.2B (ESPN, NBC, Amazon, CBS, USA Network) begin this season.
